# Index funds and ETFs

An index fund is a fund that aims to replicate the performance of a market index — such as the S&P 500 — rather than beat it. By holding the same securities as the index, it offers broad diversification at low cost. An ETF (exchange-traded fund) is a fund, often an index fund, that trades on a stock exchange like an individual stock.

## Index fund vs ETF

They overlap heavily; the differences are mostly mechanical:

- **Trading:** A traditional index mutual fund is priced once a day at market close. An ETF trades throughout the day at a live market price.
- **Minimums:** Mutual funds may require a minimum investment; ETFs can be bought by the share, or even a fractional share.
- **Taxes:** In some jurisdictions ETFs are more tax-efficient thanks to their creation/redemption mechanism.
- **Cost:** Both are typically low-cost; expense ratios for broad-market index products are often very small.

## Why they're popular

- **Diversification:** one purchase gives exposure to hundreds or thousands of companies.
- **Low cost:** passive management means lower fees than actively managed funds.
- **Simplicity:** no need to pick individual stocks or time the market.
- **Track record:** over long horizons, low-cost broad index funds have historically outperformed the majority of actively managed funds — largely because of fees.

## What to check before buying

- **Expense ratio** — the annual fee; lower is better for index products.
- **Index tracked** — what market or segment it represents (total market, large-cap, international, bonds).
- **Tracking error** — how closely it follows its index.
- **Domicile and currency** — relevant for international investors (taxes and currency exposure).

## A note on risk

Diversification reduces company-specific risk but not market risk: a broad index still falls when the whole market falls. Index investing is a long-term strategy, best paired with a time horizon that can ride out downturns.
