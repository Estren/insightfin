# Inflation and purchasing power

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time, which reduces the purchasing power of money — the same amount buys less than it did before. It is a core force in every financial system and a key reason that cash alone loses value over the long run.

## How it's measured

Most countries track inflation with a consumer price index (CPI): a basket of typical goods and services whose total price is compared period over period. Central banks often target a low, stable rate — commonly around 2% per year in many economies — to keep the system predictable.

## Why it matters for your money

- **Cash erodes:** money in a zero-interest account loses real value every year.
- **Real vs nominal return:** if an investment returns 6% while inflation is 4%, your *real* return is only about 2%. Always compare returns to inflation.
- **Wages and goals:** a salary that doesn't rise with inflation is effectively a pay cut, and long-term goals must be sized in future prices, not today's.

## How central banks respond

When inflation runs high, central banks typically raise interest rates to cool spending and borrowing; when it's too low, they cut rates to stimulate activity. These moves ripple into loan rates, savings yields, and asset prices.

## Protecting purchasing power

- Hold the emergency fund in an interest-bearing account, not idle cash.
- Invest long-term money in assets that have historically outpaced inflation, such as broad equities and certain real assets.
- Consider inflation-linked instruments where available, for example inflation-indexed government bonds.
- Revisit long-term goal amounts periodically so they stay realistic in future prices.
