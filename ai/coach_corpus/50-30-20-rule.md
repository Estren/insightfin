# The 50/30/20 rule

The 50/30/20 rule is one of the most widely used frameworks for splitting monthly take-home income. It was popularized by U.S. Senator Elizabeth Warren in *All Your Worth: The Ultimate Lifetime Money Plan* (2005).

## How it works

Split net income — after taxes and mandatory deductions — into three buckets:

- **50% needs** — essential, contractual spending: housing, basic groceries, transport, utilities (water, power, internet), health insurance, tuition, loan installments.
- **30% wants** — discretionary spending: leisure, restaurants, streaming subscriptions, travel, hobbies, non-essential clothing.
- **20% future** — emergency fund, investments, goal contributions, and extra repayment of expensive debt.

## When to apply

It's a starting point, not a rigid formula. It works best for stable income. For variable income — freelancers or project-based work — apply it to a conservative average of the last six months.

## Warning signs

- **Needs above 50%** persistently → fixed costs are heavy; revisit housing, transport, or plans.
- **Wants above 30%** recurring → discretionary spending needs a brake before debt appears.
- **Future below 20%** → the emergency fund takes too long to build, and long goals (retirement, a home) get compromised.

## Common variations

- **60/20/20** — for high cost-of-living cities (needs at 60%).
- **50/20/30** — to accelerate goals and savings (future at 30%).
- **70/20/10** — a conservative version for a debt-heavy profile, focused on paying down.

## How to measure monthly

Categorize each expense into one of the three groups and sum the percentages. If needs plus wants exceed 80%, there's little room to build wealth. A result close to 50/30/20 means a healthy budget.
