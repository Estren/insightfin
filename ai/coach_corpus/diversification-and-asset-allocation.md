# Diversification and asset allocation

Diversification is spreading investments across many assets so that no single one can sink the portfolio. Asset allocation is the higher-level decision of how to split a portfolio among asset classes — typically stocks, bonds, and cash — according to your goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance.

## Why diversify

"Don't put all your eggs in one basket." Different assets react differently to the same event: when one falls, another may hold or rise, smoothing overall returns. Diversification reduces *unsystematic* risk — the risk specific to one company or sector. It cannot remove *systematic* (market-wide) risk.

## Levels of diversification

- **Across companies** — many stocks instead of one.
- **Across sectors** — technology, healthcare, energy, consumer goods.
- **Across asset classes** — stocks, bonds, real estate, cash.
- **Across geographies** — domestic and international markets.

## Asset allocation by horizon

The longer the horizon, the more risk — and potential return — a portfolio can usually carry:

- **Long horizon (20+ years):** a higher share of stocks; volatility has time to recover.
- **Medium horizon (5–10 years):** a balanced mix of stocks and bonds.
- **Short horizon (under 3 years):** mostly cash and short-term bonds; capital preservation matters more than growth.

A common rough heuristic is to hold a stock percentage near "110 minus your age," then adjust for your own risk tolerance.

## Rebalancing

Over time, winners grow and pull your allocation away from target. Rebalancing — periodically trimming what's overweight and topping up what's underweight — restores the intended risk level and enforces "buy low, sell high" by design. Once or twice a year is usually enough.
