# Dollar-cost averaging

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals — say, monthly — regardless of price. Over time you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they're high, which smooths your average purchase price.

## How it works

Suppose you invest $300 every month:

- Month 1: price $30 → 10 shares
- Month 2: price $20 → 15 shares
- Month 3: price $25 → 12 shares

You bought more shares when the price dipped, lowering your average cost per share versus putting everything in at a single point.

## Why people use it

- **Removes timing pressure:** you don't have to guess the market's bottom.
- **Builds discipline:** automating contributions turns investing into a habit.
- **Reduces regret risk:** spreading entry points limits the impact of buying right before a drop.

## DCA vs lump-sum

If you already hold a large sum and have a long horizon, investing it all at once has historically often outperformed DCA — simply because markets rise more often than they fall, so time in the market helps. DCA's advantage is behavioral and risk-smoothing: it's easier to stick with, and it's the natural way to invest income as it arrives, such as from each paycheck.

## Practical tips

- Automate the contribution on payday so it happens before discretionary spending.
- Keep fees low — frequent small purchases only make sense with low or zero transaction costs.
- Stay consistent through downturns; that's exactly when DCA buys the most shares.
