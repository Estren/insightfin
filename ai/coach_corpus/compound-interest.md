# Compound interest

Compound interest is interest earned on both the original principal and the interest already accumulated. Unlike simple interest — calculated only on the principal — compounding makes balances grow faster over time. It is the core engine behind long-term investing.

## The formula

Future value = P × (1 + r)^n

- **P** — principal (the initial amount)
- **r** — interest rate per period
- **n** — number of periods

The exponent is why time matters more than the rate: each period compounds on a larger base than the one before.

## A simple example

$1,000 at 10% per year:

- **Simple interest:** +$100 every year → $2,000 after 10 years.
- **Compound interest:** $1,000 → $1,100 → $1,210 → … → about $2,594 after 10 years.

The gap widens dramatically over decades.

## The Rule of 72

A quick mental shortcut: divide 72 by the annual rate to estimate how many years it takes money to double.

- At 8% per year → roughly 9 years to double.
- At 6% per year → roughly 12 years.

## Why it rewards starting early

Because growth is exponential, the earliest contributions do the most work. An investor who starts at 25 and stops at 35 can end up ahead of one who starts at 35 and contributes for 30 years — the early money simply had more time to compound.

## It cuts both ways

Compounding also works against you on debt. Credit-card balances and revolving credit compound interest on unpaid amounts, which is why high-interest debt grows so quickly and should be cleared before most other financial goals.
